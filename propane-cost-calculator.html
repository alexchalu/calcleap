Understanding Propane Costs

Propane is a versatile fuel used by millions of American homes for heating, hot water, cooking, and appliances. Unlike natural gas, which is delivered via pipeline, propane requires tank storage and periodic refills or deliveries. Understanding your propane costs helps you budget effectively and identify opportunities for savings.

Propane pricing is more volatile than natural gas because it depends on wholesale markets, seasonal demand, delivery logistics, and regional supply. Prices typically peak in winter months when heating demand is highest and drop in summer when only cooking and hot water use continues.

Propane Pricing Factors

Several factors influence what you pay for propane:

  • Wholesale propane prices: Set by national and regional markets, influenced by crude oil prices and supply/demand
  • Delivery distance: Remote rural areas pay significantly more per gallon due to transportation costs
  • Tank size and ownership: Larger tanks and customer-owned tanks often receive better per-gallon pricing
  • Purchase timing: Pre-buy or locked-in summer contracts offer lower rates than emergency winter fills
  • Purchase volume: Larger orders (300+ gallons) typically cost less per gallon than small fills
  • Competition: Areas with multiple suppliers have lower prices than monopoly markets
  • Contract type: Fixed-price contracts offer stability; market-rate contracts fluctuate

Seasonal Usage Patterns

Understanding seasonal patterns helps you predict costs and plan for deliveries:

Winter months (November - March): Peak usage due to heating. A typical 2,000 sq ft home might use 80-150 gallons per month. In extremely cold climates, usage can exceed 200 gallons per month.

Summer months (June - August): Minimal usage for cooking, hot water, and dryer only. Typical usage drops to 10-30 gallons per month. This is the best time to lock in pre-buy contracts at lower prices.

Shoulder seasons (April-May, September-October): Moderate usage as occasional heating or air conditioning (if propane-powered) runs. Monthly usage typically 30-60 gallons.

Tank Ownership vs. Leasing

Company-owned tanks (most common):

  • Free or low-cost installation
  • No maintenance costs
  • Locked to one supplier (switching is difficult)
  • Typically higher per-gallon prices
  • May have minimum purchase requirements

Customer-owned tanks:

  • Upfront purchase cost: $400-3,000 depending on size
  • Shop for best propane prices from any supplier
  • Lower per-gallon costs (savings of $0.20-0.60/gallon)
  • Responsible for maintenance and inspections
  • Payback period: 2-5 years depending on usage

Reducing Propane Costs

Strategic purchasing and efficiency improvements can significantly lower your propane expenses:

Buying Strategies

  • Pre-buy contracts: Lock in summer prices (often 15-30% lower than winter) for delivery throughout the year
  • Budget plans: Fixed monthly payments spread costs evenly, avoiding large winter bills
  • Price cap contracts: Set a maximum price with ability to pay less if market drops
  • Comparison shopping: If you own your tank, get quotes from 3-5 suppliers annually
  • Bulk purchases: Fill large tanks when prices are low rather than frequent small fills
  • Group buying: Coordinate with neighbors for volume discounts

Efficiency Improvements

  • Furnace maintenance: Annual tune-ups improve efficiency by 5-15%, saving 8-23 gallons per 150-gallon winter month
  • Programmable thermostat: Reduce heating during sleeping/away hours to save 10-15% annually (120-180 gallons for 1,200 annual use)
  • Insulation upgrades: Attic, wall, and basement insulation reduce heating needs by 20-40% (240-480 gallons/year)
  • Tankless water heater: Replace tank models to save 20-30% on hot water costs (4-9 gallons/month)
  • High-efficiency appliances: 90%+ efficiency furnaces use 10-20% less propane than older 70-80% models
  • Air sealing: Seal gaps, cracks, and drafts to reduce heating losses by 10-20% (120-240 gallons/year)

Usage Optimization

  • Lower water heater temperature from 140°F to 120°F (saves 3-5 gallons/month)
  • Use cold water for laundry when possible
  • Run dishwasher only when full
  • Use lids when cooking to reduce gas consumption
  • Close fireplace damper when not in use
  • Zone heating: heat only occupied rooms

When to Schedule Propane Delivery

Proper timing prevents expensive emergency fills and ensures continuous supply:

Monitoring Your Tank Level

Most propane tanks have a gauge showing percentage full. Schedule delivery when your tank reaches:

  • 30% (winter): Schedule delivery to ensure supply during cold snaps and avoid running out
  • 20% (summer): Less urgency, but don't go below 20% to avoid sludge issues and maintain vapor pressure
  • Below 20%: Risk of running out and incurring emergency delivery fees ($75-200 extra)

Estimating When You'll Need a Refill

Track your usage patterns:

  • Note the date and tank level of each delivery
  • Calculate gallons used and days elapsed
  • Determine average gallons per day for different seasons
  • Project when your tank will reach 30% based on current usage

Example: Your 500-gallon tank was filled to 400 gallons (80%) on November 1. On January 15 (75 days later), it reads 40% (200 gallons used). Average usage: 200 ÷ 75 = 2.67 gallons/day. Current level: 40% × 500 = 200 gallons. To reach 30% (150 gallons): 50 gallons ÷ 2.67 gal/day = 19 days. Schedule delivery for early February.

Automatic Delivery vs. Will-Call

Automatic delivery:

  • Supplier monitors your usage and schedules deliveries automatically
  • Never worry about running out
  • May require you to accept delivery even if you would have preferred to wait for lower prices
  • Often required with company-owned tanks

Will-call (customer orders as needed):

  • You monitor your tank and call when you want delivery
  • More control over timing and price shopping
  • Risk of running out if you forget to check or order late
  • Emergency fees apply if you run out
  • Better for customers who actively manage energy costs

When Propane Makes Sense

  • No natural gas service: Propane is often the most practical choice for rural areas without gas lines
  • High-efficiency appliances: Modern 95%+ efficiency propane furnaces narrow the cost gap with natural gas
  • Backup power: Propane generators provide reliable backup when electricity fails
  • Cooking quality: Many chefs prefer propane/gas cooking for precise temperature control
  • Off-grid living: Propane works without electrical infrastructure

When to Consider Alternatives

  • Natural gas available: If your property can connect to natural gas, it's typically 40-60% cheaper per BTU
  • Heat pumps in mild climates: In areas with mild winters, cold-climate heat pumps offer lower operating costs
  • High electricity costs: If electricity is cheap (<$0.12/kWh), heat pumps become competitive with propane
  • Environmental concerns: Heat pumps powered by renewable electricity have lower carbon footprint

Safety and Maintenance

Propane is safe when properly handled, but requires some basic precautions:

Safety Guidelines

  • Install propane detectors near sleeping areas and propane-using appliances
  • Know the smell of propane (rotten eggs/skunk-like) and what to do if you detect it
  • If you smell gas: no flames, no switches, evacuate immediately, call supplier from outside
  • Keep tank area clear of weeds, debris, and combustible materials
  • Never store propane tanks indoors or in enclosed spaces
  • Ensure proper ventilation for all propane appliances

Regular Maintenance

  • Annual furnace inspection: Check burners, heat exchanger, venting
  • Tank inspection: Required every 10 years for above-ground, every 5 years for underground
  • Leak checks: Periodically inspect connections with soap solution (bubbles indicate leaks)
  • Regulator check: Ensure proper pressure regulation
  • Valve maintenance: Ensure tank valves operate smoothly

Environmental Considerations

Propane is a relatively clean-burning fossil fuel:

Carbon Footprint

  • Propane produces about 139 lbs CO₂ per million BTU
  • Natural gas: 117 lbs CO₂ per million BTU (16% cleaner)
  • Heating oil: 161 lbs CO₂ per million BTU (16% dirtier)
  • Electricity varies widely based on grid source (coal: 215 lbs; renewables: near 0)

Efficiency and Clean Air

  • Propane produces minimal particulate matter compared to wood or oil
  • High-efficiency propane furnaces (95%+) reduce both costs and emissions
  • Propane produces no methane leaks during distribution (unlike natural gas pipelines)
  • Qualifies for some renewable/clean energy incentives when sourced as renewable propane (from biological sources)

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does propane cost per gallon?

Propane prices vary by region, season, and delivery method. As of 2026, typical prices range from $2.00 to $4.50 per gallon. Remote areas pay more due to delivery costs. Pre-buy contracts in summer often offer lower rates than winter fill-ups when demand peaks.

How long does a 100-gallon propane tank last?

A 100-gallon propane tank (80 gallons usable) typically lasts 1-3 months for an average home using propane for heating, hot water, and cooking. In winter months with heavy heating use, it may last 4-6 weeks. Summer usage (cooking and hot water only) can extend a tank to 3-4 months.

Is it cheaper to refill or exchange propane tanks?

Refilling is almost always cheaper than exchange for small tanks. Exchanges typically give you 15 lbs of propane in a 20-lb tank, while refills give you the full 20 lbs. Refill costs average $15-20 for 20 lbs versus $20-25 for exchange (which only provides 15 lbs). For large home tanks, refills are standard.

How much propane does a house use per month?

Average monthly propane usage varies by season and appliances. Winter months (heating): 80-150 gallons. Summer months (no heating): 10-30 gallons. Annual average for a 2,000 sq ft home using propane for heating, hot water, cooking, and dryer: 800-1,200 gallons per year, or 65-100 gallons per month.

Should I own or lease my propane tank?

Leasing (supplier-owned) is simpler with no upfront cost but locks you to one supplier and higher per-gallon prices. Owning your tank costs $400-3,000 upfront but lets you shop around for better prices (typically saving $0.20-0.60/gallon). For high-usage homes, tank ownership pays for itself in 2-5 years.

When should I schedule a propane delivery?

Schedule delivery when your tank reaches 30% in winter or 20% in summer. Going below 20% risks running out and can cause tank issues. Track your usage rate (gallons per day) to predict when you'll need a refill. Emergency deliveries cost $75-200 extra, so don't wait too long.

How can I reduce my propane costs?

Key strategies include: (1) Pre-buy contracts in summer for 15-30% savings, (2) Improve home insulation and air sealing to reduce heating needs by 20-40%, (3) Maintain furnace annually for 5-15% efficiency gain, (4) Use programmable thermostat to reduce heating when away/sleeping, (5) Shop around if you own your tank, (6) Consider tank ownership for long-term savings.

How do I convert BTUs to gallons of propane?

Propane contains approximately 91,500 BTUs per gallon. To convert appliance BTU ratings to gallons per hour: divide BTU/hour by 91,500. For example, a 75,000 BTU/hr furnace uses 0.82 gallons per hour (75,000 ÷ 91,500 = 0.82). Multiply by hours of operation to estimate consumption.
