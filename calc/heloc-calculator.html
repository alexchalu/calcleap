Understanding Home Equity Lines of Credit (HELOCs)

A Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC) is a revolving credit line secured by your home's equity. Unlike a traditional home equity loan that gives you a lump sum upfront, a HELOC works more like a credit card—you can borrow, repay, and borrow again up to your credit limit during the "draw period." This flexibility makes HELOCs popular for home renovations, debt consolidation, and other major expenses.

How HELOC Payments Work: Two Distinct Phases

Phase 1: Draw Period (Typically 5-10 Years)

During the draw period, you can borrow money up to your credit limit and make interest-only payments on the amount you've drawn. You're not required to pay down the principal balance during this phase. For example, if you have a $100,000 HELOC at 7.5% interest and draw $50,000, your monthly payment during the draw period would be approximately $312 (interest-only on $50,000).

Phase 2: Repayment Period (Typically 10-20 Years)

Once the draw period ends, you can no longer borrow from the HELOC. You must now pay back both principal and interest, which typically causes your monthly payment to increase significantly. Using the same example, your payment would jump from $312 to approximately $403 per month for a 20-year repayment period.

Payment Shock: Many homeowners are unprepared for the payment increase when transitioning from the draw period to repayment period. In the example above, that's a 29% payment increase. For larger balances, the shock can be even more dramatic.

HELOC vs. Home Equity Loan: Key Differences

HELOC (Line of Credit):

  • Variable interest rate (tied to Prime Rate)
  • Borrow as needed up to credit limit
  • Interest-only payments during draw period
  • Flexible access to funds for ongoing projects
  • Payments can fluctuate with interest rate changes
  • Best for: Uncertain costs, multiple projects over time, emergency fund backup

Home Equity Loan (Lump Sum):

  • Fixed interest rate
  • One-time lump sum at closing
  • Fixed monthly payments from day one
  • Predictable payment schedule
  • Cannot borrow more without refinancing
  • Best for: One-time expenses with known costs, debt consolidation, predictable budgeting

Current HELOC Interest Rates & How They Work

Most HELOCs have variable interest rates tied to the Prime Rate plus a margin (typically 0.5% to 3.5% depending on your creditworthiness). As of early 2026, typical HELOC rates range from 7% to 10%. When the Federal Reserve adjusts rates, your HELOC rate will change accordingly, affecting your monthly payment.

Example of rate impact: On a $50,000 HELOC balance:

  • At 7% interest: $291/month (interest-only)
  • At 9% interest: $375/month (interest-only)
  • That's a $84/month ($1,008/year) difference from a 2% rate increase

How Much Can You Borrow with a HELOC?

Most lenders allow you to borrow up to 80-90% of your home's appraised value, minus what you owe on your first mortgage. This is called the Combined Loan-to-Value (CLTV) ratio.

Example calculation:

  • Home value: $400,000
  • First mortgage balance: $250,000
  • Maximum CLTV: 85%
  • Maximum total debt: $400,000 × 85% = $340,000
  • Available HELOC limit: $340,000 - $250,000 = $90,000

Higher credit scores and lower debt-to-income ratios can help you qualify for higher CLTV limits and better interest rates.

Smart HELOC Strategies

1. Make principal payments during the draw period: You're not required to, but paying down principal during the draw period can dramatically reduce your repayment-period payment and total interest costs.

2. Refinance before the repayment period: If interest rates drop or you want more predictable payments, consider refinancing to a fixed-rate home equity loan or cash-out refinance before your repayment period begins.

3. Use it as an emergency fund backup: Keep a HELOC open with zero or low balance as a financial safety net. You'll typically pay no interest if you don't draw funds, though some lenders charge small annual fees ($50-100).

4. Track your spending closely: Because HELOCs are revolving credit, it's easy to overborrow. Set a strict budget and track every draw to avoid getting in over your head.

5. Consider the tax implications: HELOC interest is tax-deductible only if you use the funds to "buy, build, or substantially improve" your home. Interest on funds used for other purposes (debt consolidation, car purchases, etc.) is not deductible.

HELOC Costs Beyond Interest

While HELOCs often have lower upfront costs than home equity loans, watch for these fees:

  • Application fee: $0-$500
  • Appraisal fee: $300-$500
  • Annual fee: $0-$100 (some lenders waive this if you maintain a minimum balance)
  • Closing costs: $0-$1,000 (many lenders waive these to compete for customers)
  • Early closure fee: $250-$500 if you close the HELOC within the first 2-3 years
  • Inactivity fee: Some lenders charge if you don't draw funds within a certain period

When a HELOC Makes Sense

  • You have ongoing home renovation projects with uncertain costs
  • You want a financial safety net for emergencies
  • You're consolidating high-interest debt and plan to pay it off quickly
  • You're comfortable with variable interest rates and potential payment fluctuations
  • You have discipline to not overspend just because credit is available

When to Avoid a HELOC

  • You have a one-time expense with a known cost (get a home equity loan instead)
  • You're worried about rising interest rates
  • Your income is unstable or you're close to retirement
  • You tend to overspend when credit is easily accessible
  • You have less than 20% equity in your home

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens when the HELOC draw period ends?
When the draw period ends (typically after 5-10 years), you can no longer borrow from the HELOC. Your monthly payment will increase because you must now pay both principal and interest instead of interest-only. For example, a $50,000 balance at 7.5% interest would go from $312/month (interest-only) to approximately $403/month over a 20-year repayment period. Some lenders may offer to convert your HELOC to a fixed-rate loan at this point.
Can my HELOC interest rate change?
Yes, most HELOCs have variable interest rates tied to the Prime Rate. When the Federal Reserve raises or lowers rates, your HELOC rate will adjust accordingly, usually within one billing cycle. This means your monthly payment can increase or decrease over time. Some lenders offer fixed-rate conversion options or rate caps that limit how high your rate can go. If you want payment certainty, consider a fixed-rate home equity loan instead.
How much equity do I need to qualify for a HELOC?
Most lenders require at least 15-20% equity in your home, though some may lend up to 85-90% of your home's value (CLTV). For example, if your home is worth $400,000 and you owe $250,000, you have $150,000 in equity (37.5%). With an 85% CLTV limit, you could potentially qualify for a HELOC up to $90,000 ($340,000 total debt minus $250,000 mortgage). Higher credit scores and lower debt-to-income ratios help you qualify for larger amounts and better rates.
Is HELOC interest tax deductible?
HELOC interest is tax deductible only if you use the funds to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the home that secures the loan. This includes major renovations, additions, or repairs that increase your home's value. Interest on HELOC funds used for other purposes—such as paying off credit cards, buying a car, or funding a vacation—is NOT tax deductible. Keep detailed records of how you use HELOC funds and consult a tax professional for guidance specific to your situation.
What credit score do I need for a HELOC?
Most lenders require a minimum credit score of 620-680 for HELOC approval, though the best rates typically go to borrowers with scores of 740 or higher. Your credit score affects both approval and the interest rate margin added to the Prime Rate. For example, a borrower with a 760 score might get Prime + 0.5% (7.5% total), while a 660 score might get Prime + 2.5% (9.5% total). Improving your credit score before applying can save you thousands in interest over the life of the HELOC.
Can I pay off my HELOC early without penalty?
Most HELOCs allow early payoff without penalty, but check your specific loan agreement. Some lenders charge an early closure fee ($250-$500) if you close the HELOC entirely within the first 2-3 years. This is different from simply paying down the balance—you can usually pay extra toward principal anytime without penalty. Making extra payments during the draw period can significantly reduce your total interest costs and lower your payment when you enter the repayment period.
What happens to my HELOC if I sell my house?
You must pay off your HELOC when you sell your home, just like your primary mortgage. The HELOC balance will be deducted from your sale proceeds at closing. For example, if you sell for $450,000, owe $200,000 on your first mortgage and $50,000 on your HELOC, you'll net approximately $200,000 minus closing costs and realtor fees. If you're refinancing instead of selling, you can usually pay off the HELOC with the new loan proceeds or keep it open if the new lender allows subordination.
Should I choose a HELOC or a home equity loan?
Choose a HELOC if you need ongoing access to funds, have variable or uncertain expenses, or want flexibility to borrow and repay multiple times. Choose a home equity loan if you have a one-time expense with a known cost, want predictable fixed payments, or are concerned about rising interest rates. HELOCs are ideal for long-term home renovations or as an emergency fund backup. Home equity loans are better for debt consolidation, major one-time purchases, or when you need payment certainty for budgeting.
